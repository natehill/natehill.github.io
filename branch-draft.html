Draft for Branch Magazine

Maintaining a vibrant creative practice is an essential part of my work as the leader of an organization working to address big problems like digital equity, the preservation of history, and soon, climate justice. Pairing work that is measured in hours, days, and fiscal quarters with intentionally slow, process-driven crafts that use tactile, physical materials allows me to engage in a generative approach for the discovery of contextually unique solutions to complex predicaments. By taking 2 years to transform a scavenged piece of wood into a refined object representing an idea, I make room for the process to alter the idea itself. The pictures below show the process and some of the byproducts of me making a box named Triploid Hemerocallis.

These carved plywood panels are made with a handheld grinder, and not with a computer-aided router. I call them Margulis Panels because they are inspired by the work of the biologist Lynn Margulis, who discovered and popularized the concept of endosymbiosis. The process by which these panels are made explores growth, combination, and mergers of form. I lay out circles at random, overlap them, join them, and then begin to carve. In the process a new form emerges, one that is rooted in the origin shapes but has also taken on a new life of its own.
